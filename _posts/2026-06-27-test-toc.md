## Leathercraft: Getting Started

Leathercraft is a rewarding hobby that blends creativity with craftsmanship. Here are some foundational topics to explore.

### Choosing Your First Leather

- **Vegetable-tanned leather** — best for tooling, stamping, and carving
- **Chrome-tanned leather** — softer, more supple, great for bags and garments
- **Split leather** — affordable, used for lining and suede

### Essential Tools

| Tool | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| **Craft knife / rotary cutter** | Cutting leather precisely |
| **Stitching chisels** | Punching evenly spaced holes |
| **Saddler's needles & thread** | Hand stitching (saddle stitch) |
| **Edge beveler** | Smoothing rough edges |
| **Burnishing tool** | Polishing and sealing edges |

### Basic Techniques

#### Cutting

Always cut on a self-healing mat using a sharp blade. Dull blades tear leather fibers.

#### Stitching

The **saddle stitch** is the gold standard for leather goods. Two needles, one thread — each stitch locks in place, so a single broken thread won't unravel the whole seam.

#### Edge Finishing

1. Bevel the edges to remove sharp corners
2. Sand with progressively finer grits
3. Apply edge paint or gum tragacanth
4. Burnish with a wooden slicker or canvas

## Intermediate Topics

Once you've mastered the basics, there's plenty more to explore.

### Pattern Making

Drafting your own patterns opens up endless possibilities. Start with simple geometries (wallets, card holders) and work up to complex 3D shapes like bags and backpacks.

### Dyeing and Antiquing

Alcohol-based dyes penetrate deeply, while oil-based dyes sit more on the surface. Antiquing gels settle into tooling impressions for a vintage look.

### Hardware Installation

Snaps, rivets, and zippers each require specific tools and techniques. A good snap setter and rivet anvil are worth the investment.
